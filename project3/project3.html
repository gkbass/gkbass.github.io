ORBIT OF UNITED STATES AND CHINESE SATELLITES AROUND EARTH

To represent China’s expansion of their “space-based reconnaissance, communications, and navigation systems in terms of the number of satellites, the breadth of their capability, and their applications for use” (Director of National Intelligence, 2018, p. 13) we examine the Union of Concerned Scientists Space Security space security database. Scientists collected open-source data on 2,062 satellites currently orbiting Earth, including country of origin, purpose, and other operational details. To exhibit Chinese, Russian, and United States influence in space and counter space, the data was compiled and visualized on an interactive globe that accurately displays satellites in their respective orbit, altitude from earth, and from each other. Because the database included the five-digit unique satellite catalog number also known as NORAD, we were able to use this data to generate TLE-3 formatted data. The Two Line Elements (TLE) is a standard mathematical model that describes a satellite’s orbit using the position and velocity at a given point in time. To calculate the TLE-3 for Chinese, Russia, and United States satellites identified in the database, we used Space Track — the primary distributor of U.S. Department of Defense 2-line orbital elements and related data. Space-Track “promote[s] space flight safety, protection of the space environment and the peaceful use of space worldwide by sharing space situational awareness services and information with U.S. and international satellite owners/operators, academia and other entities.” Each satellite is projected at the “latest epoch” according to Space-Track. The area of each circle is inversely proportional to the satellite’s altitude from the surface of the earth, so the closer and faster orbiting satellites exhibit a larger radius. The visualization was created based on the work of Vasco Asturiano and Mike Bostock.

THE UNITED STATES and CHINESE SATELLITE LANDSCAPE

Next we delve into an indepth analysis of the Chinese/U.S. Satellite landscape where we compare and contrast the number of operational satellites, satellites users, and the purpose of the satellites.

Since 2007, China and the United States have steadily increased the number of satellites launched with a dramatic surge in 2017 and 2018. The spike in satellite launches by the United States in 2017 is due to the new 2k and 3p Dove Planet Labs constellations. The the main purposes of satellites for both China and the United States is for earth observation, communication, and technology development. The United States based company Planet Labs operates and owns 23.86% or 215 of all U.S. registered satellites, followed by Iridium Communications Inc. with 8.88% or 80 registered satellites. In China, the People’s Liberation Army operates and owns 20.74% or 62 of the total 299 Chinese satellites, followed by the Chinese Defense Ministry in control of 13.38% or 40 of registered satellites. Of the six largest satellite operators and owners, the Chinese government controls 52.85% compared to only 15.32% of satellites in the United States directly controlled by the government. The number of classified U.S or Chinese satellites remain unknown. Of the six largest U.S. satellite operators, three of them are private companies (Planet Labs, Iridium Communications Inc., and Spire Global Inc.) with the other three operators the Department of Defense, National Aeronautics and Space Administration, and the National Reconnaissance Office. The number of satellites operated by the Chinese (122 satellites) and U.S. (164 satellites) government is surprisingly proportional, but there are about three times more commercial satellite users than government users in the United States.

The United States possesses more access to “space-derived information services” (Director of National Intelligence, 2018, p. 13) but China is rapidly expanding their space program to meet their growing needs for more in-depth 24-7 intelligence imagery, weather forecasting, communication capabilities, and position and navigation intelligence. Around the world, countries have addressed the security concerns of BeiDou GPS satellite capabilities. The recent and continued launch of the BeiDou navigation satellites will create a network by the end of 2020 that will consist of more than 30 satellites to give global communication and navigation coverage for both commercial and military applications. Understanding the space-satellite landscape is vital in accurately assessing future threats China poses to United States space capabilities around the world.
